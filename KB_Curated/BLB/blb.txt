Bacterial Leaf Blight (BLB)

Bangla Name
Patapora Rog

Impact on Rice Yield
Bacterial leaf blight can reduce rice yield by an average of around 30% each year. This disease can appear in the field throughout the year. The level of damage varies depending on the rice variety.

Cause of the Disease
The disease is caused by a type of bacterium.

Visual Symptoms
Seedlings droop or wilt, turn grayish-green to yellow or brown, dry out.
Milky or pus-like fluid oozes out from the plant.
Water-soaked, pale-yellow or pale-brown or yellow streaks along the leaf margins.
Bluish water-soaked lesions or long streaks at the leaf tips or along the leaf edges.
Light brown or yellow lesions extend downward from the leaf tip.
The entire leaf scorch and turn grayish or straw-colored, resembling dry hay.

Symptoms Details
Bacterial leaf blight shows three distinct types of symptoms on rice leaves and seedlings:

a) Kresek Symptom
1) This symptom usually appears at the seedling or tillering stage.
2) Infected seedlings initially droop, turn yellow, dry out, and eventually die.
3) A milky or pus-like fluid oozes out from the plant, and water-soaked lesions appear on the leaves.

b) Pale Yellow Leaf
1) Water-soaked, pale-yellow streaks develop along the leaf margins. Sometimes, young leaves turn pale brown or yellow and gradually die.
2) This symptom is more noticeable when there is a large difference between day and night temperatures (about 8–10°C).

c) Leaf Burn Symptom
1) This can appear at the tillering stage or any later growth stage.
2) Initially, bluish water-soaked lesions or long streaks appear at the leaf tips or along the leaf edges.
3) The lesions gradually turn light brown or yellow and extend downward from the leaf tip.
4) Eventually, parts or the entire leaf scorch and turn grayish or straw-colored, resembling dry hay.

Favorable Conditions for Disease Development
1) Cultivation of susceptible rice varieties.
2) Excessive application of urea fertilizer.
3) Injury to roots or plants during transplanting.
4) High temperatures (26–30°C).
5) High humidity (above 70%).
6) Stormy and rainy weather.

Disease Transmission
1) The bacteria survive for some time in crop residues, wet soil, and irrigation water.
2) The pathogen enters the plant through wounds.
3) Strong winds, rain, and irrigation water help spread the disease.
4) The bacteria can also survive on Arali grass (Leersia species), which helps in spreading the disease.

Management Practices

Management can be done in two ways:

a) Preventive Measures (Before Disease Occurrence)
1) Resistant Varieties:
i) Boro season: BR2, BR14, BR16, BRRI dhan19, BRRI dhan45, BRRI dhan50
ii) Aus season: BR26, BRRI dhan27
iii) Aman season: BR4, BRRI dhan32, BRRI dhan33, BRRI dhan37, BRRI dhan38, BRRI dhan40, BRRI dhan41, BRRI dhan42, BRRI dhan44, BRRI dhan49

2) Apply fertilizers in balanced amounts and split urea fertilizer into three applications.

B) Control Measures (In Infected Fields)
1) Do not apply urea fertilizer after storms or heavy rains.
2) Stop top dressing of urea immediately after disease symptoms appear.
3) Apply 5 kg of potash fertilizer per bigha (≈0.13 ha) after disease is detected.
4) For fields with kresek symptoms, drain the field and keep it dry for 5–10 days, then re-irrigate.
5) Burn infected stubble and straw after harvest to destroy the source of infection.
